# Doctorina MedBench: End-to-End Evaluation of Agent-Based Medical AI

# Abstract 

We present Doctorina MedBench, a comprehensive evaluation framework for agent-based medical AI based on the simulation of realistic physician–patient interactions. Unlike traditional medical benchmarks that rely on solving standardized test questions, the proposed approach models a multi-step clinical dialogue in which either a physician or an AI system must collect medical history, analyze attached materials (including laboratory reports, images, and medical documents), formulate differential diagnoses, and provide personalized recommendations. System performance is evaluated using the D.O.T.S. metric, which consists of four components: Diagnosis, Observations/Investigations, Treatment, and Step Count, enabling assessment of both clinical correctness and dialogue efficiency. The system also incorporates a multi-level testing and quality monitoring architecture designed to detect model degradation during both development and deployment. The framework supports safety-oriented trap cases, category-based random sampling of clinical scenarios, and full regression testing. The dataset currently contains more than 1,000 clinical cases covering over 750 diagnoses. The universality of the evaluation metrics allows the framework to be used not only to assess medical AI systems, but also to evaluate physicians and support the development of clinical reasoning skills. Our results suggest that simulation of clinical dialogue may provide a more realistic assessment of clinical competence compared to traditional examination-style benchmarks. 1

# 1 Introduction 

The rapid advancement of AI and the mounting deficit of healthcare professionals have led to widespread adoption of AI as a personal health consultant. Despite early skepticism from the medical community, evidence indicates that physicians are increasingly utilizing Large Language Models (LLMs) in their clinical practice. Specifically, in 2024, 66% of doctors reported using AI, marking a 78% increase compared to 2023 [1]. This trajectory is reflected in the burgeoning number of publications evaluating the performance of AI in medical tasks. In recent years, numerous studies have investigated AI both as a tool for solving specific medical tasks and for its ability to substitute for specialist consultations. For example, this includes its application in patient information and education [2], the capacity to handle specific tasks such as personalized medication selection [3], the development of decision-support models [4], or fulfilling the role of a physician for the patient [5, 6]. As a result, the question of assessing the quality of such diagnostics and the stability of models is becoming increasingly relevant. Various performance evaluation systems have been tested; although LLM models have long been able to solve standard medical tests [7], this does not allow for confident extrapolation of such data to real clinical cases [8]. Therefore, early methods for comparing AI in medicine relied on benchmarking against expert opinions, which created certain difficulties. These challenges included the limited productivity of humans who, unlike LLMs, cannot process hundreds of cases in a short period of time, as well as the presence of the human factor, which can lead to incorrect interpretation of AI responses. Consequently, it was concluded that the consensus of multiple specialists is necessary; however, this approach significantly limits throughput. These constraints prompted the development of fundamentally new AI quality assessment systems designed to address the challenge of accurately evaluating AI performance across various medical scenarios. Furthermore, such systems could even facilitate the training of students in clinical reasoning by simulating realistic clinical cases and patient interactions [9, 10, 11]. However, despite the significant human and economic resources invested in the development of such systems, they do not fully address all the challenges encountered in creating an evaluation model for an AI physician. In particular, these approaches only allow for an assessment of the overall performance of an AI physician at a specific point in time. Furthermore, they remain highly resource-intensive, especially during the development of new models and the need for continuous testing of even minor modifications. Given that Doctorina aims to create a fully functional, freely accessible AI physician, there is a need to address three interrelated tasks within a single system. First, determining the overall quality level of the AI physician during its development and subsequent improvement. Second, verifying the stability and performance of the AI physician in real-time. Third, the timely identification and localization of emerging issues, including errors in file processing, user message translation, and the general logic of the system. 22 System Overview 

# 2.1 General Principles of the AI Doctor 

The developed AI physician, Doctorina, is a proactive system designed to perform diagnostic assessment and generate valid recommendations. The system fully replicates the behavior of a real physician during patient interactions and is neither a symptom checker nor an adapted system to answer specific medical questions. The mechanism is capable of not only receiving text messages from patients but also processing various types of attachments—ranging from photographs of body parts to lab results and medical reports—consolidating all information into a single block for subsequent analysis. Accordingly, Doctorina asks questions guided by clinical reasoning based on available information, gradually narrowing down the range of potential diseases with each new patient response. Doctorina’s objective is to collect all clinically relevant patient data, not only to establish a diagnosis but also to provide evidence-based recommendations. This ensures more than just the formulation of a probable diagnosis; it facilitates a multi-stage process of identifying the primary diagnosis and comorbidities through the sequential exclusion of options from the differential diagnosis. Additionally, the system accounts for factors that influence the final recommendations, such as family history. Based on this, the system generates final recommendations that consider all individual characteristics. The final recommendations include an explanation of the patient’s condition, as well as a personalized diagnostic plan to confirm or clarify the diagnosis and subsequent treatment. Furthermore, it provides for condition monitoring with protocols for what to do in case of deterioration, alongside personalized lifestyle advice covering nutrition, physical activity, habits and sleep hygiene. 

# 2.2 Simulation of the AI-Patient 

During the development of a multi-component application such as a generative AI physician, it was found that traditional evaluation methodologies do not provide a comprehensive and accurate analysis of its effectiveness. In contrast to standardized examinations, real-world patients rarely provide all pertinent information to medical professionals spontaneously. Instead, clinicians must actively elicit details, clarify misconceptions, and guide the patient through the history-taking process before formulating a diagnostic hypothesis or recommendations. To address this gap, we developed simulation scenarios that more accurately replicate the dynamics of real-world clinical interactions. Recent benchmark studies have similarly emphasized that realistic assessment of medical LLMs should extend beyond static question answering to interactive task environments and rare off-guideline clinical scenarios, where performance depends on sequential questioning, contextual interpretation, and appropriate use of available evidence [9], [12]. To evaluate the diagnostic and therapeutic efficacy of the system under realistic conditions, we model physician-patient interactions using a structured testing mechanism. The simulation operates as a step-by-step cycle with a defined iteration limit: it begins by adding introductory ”patient” information and optional attachments to the conversation state, after which the AI physician’s responses are repeatedly fed back into the cycle until the dialogue is explicitly marked as complete or the predefined step limit is exhausted. This mechanism allows for control over dialogue length and ensures that completion is determined either by the model itself, when it explicitly reports primary diagnosis, or as a result of a specified timeout. The patient behavior is intentionally designed to approximate real clinical consultations, in which clinically relevant information is often revealed only in response to targeted questioning. This makes the benchmark sensitive not only to final diagnostic accuracy, but also to the completeness and safety of the physician model’s history-taking strategy. The patient’s behavior is intentionally restricted to mimic real clinical consultations. In particular, the simulated patient does not disclose all clinically relevant details spontaneously; instead, the AI physician must obtain them through targeted history-taking. This allows the benchmark to measure not only the correctness of the final diagnosis and recommendations, but also the completeness and safety of the information-gathering process. The virtual patient is realized as a separate LLM agent governed by a dedicated system prompt. Each benchmark case is stored as a structured record. During simulation, the patient agent is conditioned on this case record and the current dialogue history. The agent is explicitly constrained to behave like a real patient. It answers only the question asked, keeps responses concise, does not reveal clinically relevant details unless they are specifically elicited, and does not introduce facts that are absent from the case record. It may paraphrase case facts in natural language, but it is not allowed to expand them with unsupported symptoms, exposures, diagnoses, or test results. If the physician 3asks about information not represented in the case, the patient produces an uncertain or negative reply instead of hallucinating. This architecture preserves the linguistic flexibility of an LLM-driven dialogue while maintaining case-level factual control. Consequently, performance depends on the physician model’s history-taking strategy rather than on accidental over-disclosure by the simulated patient.

The system logs every step, preserving attachments, tracking completion flags, and recording model inputs and outputs for subsequent analysis. Upon completion of a simulated dialogue, the testing mechanism performs a multidimensional evaluation. Four asynchronous tasks evaluate the dialogue’s completeness, the quality of recommended treatment and history-taking, and the correctness of prescribed tests and examinations in parallel. The results are structured into an evaluation record in the DOTS format (Diagnosis, Observations / Investigations, Treatment, Step count), which can be used to determine ”pass/fail” status for ”trap” cases and for analyzing regression statistics. Test cases and evaluation logic are housed in a separate module, ensuring that experimental checks cannot influence real patient interactions. This separation also facilitates safe parallelization and rapid re-runs within continuous integration or real-time monitoring frameworks. Future improvements may include explicit modeling of, for example, emergency and routine consultations. Additionally, stricter step limits for high-complexity scenarios and expanded use of attachments with structured data are planned to stress-test the system’s ability to extract and correctly utilize information. Furthermore, the system is flexible, allowing for the simulation of not only the physician-patient dialogue but also the definition of the patient’s behavioral style. Thus, the simulated patient can not only provide answers to questions but also provide them with excessive information, insist on specific diagnoses or deny their possibility, and ask clarifying questions. This is frequently encountered in real-world medical practice when a patient attempts to convince a doctor of something. As a result, AI patient can mimic behavior not only within a predefined ”medical case” but also correspond to various behavioral archetypes of real people. After receiving final recommendations, a scenario is possible where the patient initiates communication to clarify information or report ”forgotten” facts, necessitating adjustments to previously issued prescriptions. 43 Evaluation Framework 

# 3.1 Core Metrics (D.O.T.S.) 

• Diagnosis (D) — accuracy and completeness of the diagnostic solution. 

• Observations / Investigations (O) — validity of recommendations for conducting surveys. 

• Treatment (T) — safety, adequacy and therapeutic logic. 

• Step Count (S) — the number of steps in the dialogue required to make a diagnosis and prescribe treatment. 

• Pass thresholds defined for each level (e.g. no critical errors, stage deviations ≤ 20%). 

Figure 2: D.O.T.S. The primary metric for evaluating the performance of the AI physician model is the assessment of outcomes from a simulated dialogue between a virtual AI patient and an AI physician. The AI patient is provided with baseline data (an introductory prompt) that initiates the interaction, along with a repository of responses to potential inquiries within the simulated scenario covering secondary symptoms, medical history (anamnesis), family history, diagnostic test results, allergies, and current medications. The process culminates in the establishment of a diagnosis by the AI physician and the provision of primary clinical recommendations. The AI patient is trained to respond exclusively to the specific questions posed, without volunteering unsolicited information, which closely mirrors real-world clinical practice. This includes providing data from laboratory and other diagnostic methods, both at the onset of the interaction and during its course upon the physician’s request. Additionally, the AI patient can pose clarifying questions to the physician, such as requesting the explanation of complex medical terminology. Following the formulation of the diagnosis and recommendations, key performance indicators (KPIs) are evaluated—specifically: Diagnosis (D), Observations/Investigations (O), Treatment (T), and Step Count (S)—and compared against the gold standard benchmarks for the given clinical situation. The Diagnosis (D) indicator comprises two independent metrics: the primary diagnosis itself (the chief complaint or reason for the virtual patient’s visit) in its textual form, and the corresponding ICD-10 code. The use of ICD-10 provides a standardized international framework for representing diagnoses and facilitates comparison across cases. Accurate mapping between standardized medical codes and clinical concepts constitutes an important and separate dimension of medical LLM evaluation [13]. The application of two or more codes for a single clinical scenario is permissible. The inclusion of the second metric (ICD-10) accounts for the fact that professional medical literature often employs diverse terminology to describe the same pathology; for instance, ”pelvic prolapse,” ”pelvic organ prolapse,” and ”cystocele” may describe the identical clinical situation and correspond to the same ICD-10 code. The system is verified against both the textual description and the ICD-10 code; a result is deemed unsatisfactory only if both indicators fail to match simultaneously. Partial discrepancies may be attributed to either synonymy in diagnostic terminology or the availability of multiple valid ICD-10 codes for primary medical encounters 5(e.g., cases involving non-specific symptoms where diagnostic etiology is being clarified alongside the initiation of treatment). Furthermore, the differential diagnosis list for each clinical scenario is evaluated case-by-case, specifically measuring the diagnostic accuracy of the Top-3 and Top-5. Observations / Investigations (O) — This module evaluates the adherence of the model’s diagnostic recom-mendations to established clinical protocols. Beyond baseline scenarios, complex medical cases may include data that require adjustments to the diagnostic plan, such as partially completed tests or incidental abnormal findings unrelated to the primary complaint but requiring further investigation. An example of the latter would be an abnormal cervical cytology result in a patient with a gastrointestinal disorder. This indicator provides an integral assessment of diagnostic precision and is composed of three logically distinct components: mandatory tests (should be recommended), optional or permissible tests (can be recommended), and a penalty system for redundant or irrelevant orders (unexpected penalty). This structure reflects clinical reality, where investigations are categorized as essential for diagnosis and patient safety, elective yet justified, or, conversely, unjustified and excessive. The first group (mandatory investigations) forms the core of the evaluation. Each element is assigned a weight reflecting its diagnostic significance. The model receives points only for investigations explicitly mentioned in its recommendations. Omitting a mandatory test results in the automatic loss of its corresponding weight from the maximum possible score, thereby objectively documenting any incompleteness in the diagnostic search. The second group (optional investigations) includes tests that may be appropriate in certain clinical contexts but are not strictly required. These elements are assigned a weight of zero. Consequently, their presence does not improve the final score, nor does it diminish it, allowing the model to exhibit clinical variability without risk of penalty. The third component (unexpected penalty system) is triggered when the model recommends investigations that are neither mandatory nor optional. Each such entry reduces the final score by a fixed amount, reflecting the real-world clinical issue of over-testing, which leads to increased healthcare costs, patient burden, and potential procedural risks. The final output is a percentage score (0–100%) representing the accuracy of the diagnostic recommendations for a specific case. The Treatment (T) block is analyzed using similar principles, with the addition of a Critical Condition override. These are predefined criteria which, if triggered, result in the case automatically receiving a score of zero and a special flag. These conditions identify ”gross medical errors” and are used to monitor for severe violations, the occurrence of which is modeled artificially. For instance, recommending Amoxicillin to a patient with a documented penicillin allergy would trigger this override. The purpose of this indicator is to ensure rigorous control over high-stakes clinical errors, moving beyond simple monitoring of over- or under-prescription. Step Count (S) represents the number of inquiries initiated by the AI physician before reaching a final diagnosis and providing recommendations. This metric does not assess the completeness of the clinical interview (captured by the indicators described above), but rather serves as a behavioral audit of the model. Large variations in the number of steps may indicate redundant questioning, excessive detail, or improper grouping of clinical inquiries. A deviation exceeding 25% from the gold standard benchmark, either within a single case or across identical scenarios is considered a red flag suggesting potential inconsistencies in the model’s diagnostic reasoning process. # 3.2 Evaluator Implementation 

Following the completion of each simulated dialogue, the benchmark utilizes an asynchronous evaluation pipeline comprising four parallel assessment modules: (1) dialogue-level clinical evaluation, (2) history-taking assessment, (3) treatment-condition verification, and (4) diagnostic-workup checking. These components collectively quantify performance across multiple dimensions, including Diagnosis, Differential, and ICD-10 Accuracy, as well as Treatment Proficiency, Question Coverage, and safety-critical pass conditions. The final aggregation of metrics is executed through a deterministic, case-based framework. The evaluation engine employs a structured ”LLM-as-a-Judge” architecture. To ensure objectivity, the system avoids free-form assessment in favor of a schema-validated extraction process. Clinical entities—including primary and differential diagnoses, recommended investigations, and therapeutic interventions—are identified through constrained LLM calls using Pydantic-based response formats. Furthermore, ICD-10 codes are normalized from structured medical data and evaluated using algorithmic code-level matching rules. These extracted outputs are subsequently processed by deterministic scoring functions that implement weighted diagnostic metrics, mandatory inquiry coverage, and safety-critical overrides. 6To mitigate evaluator hallucinations and ensure auditability, the pipeline mandates explicit evidence extraction, requiring the inclusion of direct dialogue quotations or a ”no evidence” label where information is absent. The extraction scope is strictly confined to final recommendations to avoid confounding with intermediate reasoning. By converting natural language inputs into schema-constrained structured data prior to scoring, this hybrid design balances the linguistic flexibility of LLMs with the reproducibility and rigor required for standardized medical benchmarking. 74 Multi-Level Testing Protocol and Continuous Monitoring 

Continuous Quality Assurance is critical for maintaining the safety and diagnostic reliability of a generative AI doctor system. To this end, we have implemented a real-time monitoring layer that operates continuously and in parallel with the production environment. This layer is designed to rapidly identify performance degradations, often within minutes, while remaining completely isolated from patient interactions. This layer is intended for the early detection of performance degradation, which can arise in the Doctorina archi-tecture for several reasons. First, updates to base models or agent routing can alter dialogue behavior, affecting the thoroughness of history taking and the correctness of clinical reasoning. Second, changes in input data—including new laboratory report formats, image quality, or user language variability—can reduce the accuracy of extracting clinically significant information from attachments. Third, degradation may manifest as logic regression in differ-ential diagnosis or in the formulation of recommendations, as reflected in DOTS metrics (Diagnosis, Observations, Treatment, Steps). Finally, infrastructure factors (service latency, module routing errors, computational resource overloads) can disrupt the dialogue cycle sequence and impact the successful completion of clinical scenarios. These cases are executed at a fixed but adaptive frequency—typically daily—with dynamic rescheduling for high-priority ”trap cases” or recently failed tests. This strategy ensures system sensitivity to sudden degradations while minimizing computational costs. Probes are performed across all active model versions, regions, and languages, providing immediate and comparable performance snapshots. Each run generates a structured set of metrics, including diagnostic accuracy, the appropriateness of recom-mended examinations, the adequacy and safety of treatment, and the efficiency of dialogue steps. Additional metrics track hallucinations, source-grounding errors, attachment parsing quality, routing stability, and translation robust-ness. These results are automatically aggregated and stratified by clinical category, language, and demographic context, enabling the early detection of performance drift within specific categories [14]. A key element of the system is the hierarchical escalation mechanism (Fig. 3). If a trap case fails, particularly in high-risk safety scenarios, the monitoring layer initiates a full Level 3 regression run using the entire case bank. If these comprehensive tests confirm reproducible degradation, the system automatically notifies clinical and engineering teams and blocks the promotion of the affected model version until fixes and re-validation are complete. This cascading architecture ensures that transient fluctuations do not trigger unnecessary shutdowns, while confirmed failures receive immediate attention. 

Figure 3: Real-time Monitoring System Architecture. Short clinical ”trap” test scenarios are executed in parallel with the production system and evaluated using DOTS metrics. Upon detection of anomalies, a comprehensive regression run is triggered. If performance degradation is confirmed, the system automatically dispatches notifica-tions, blocks model promotion, and initiates remediation processes before returning the model to the monitoring cycle. Anomaly detection operates on both short-term (sliding one-hour window) and longer twenty-four-hour inter-vals, comparing metrics against a seven-day median baseline. This approach enables the identification of statistically significant regressions rather than random outliers. Repeated probe executions are used to assess variance and confirm instability before escalation, thereby enhancing the reliability of the monitoring circuit. All monitoring activities are conducted in an isolated evaluation environment, strictly separated from production data streams to guarantee confidentiality and eliminate any impact on the user experience. System outputs are visualized in a live dashboard displaying pass rates, severity-weighted safety indices, step efficiency trends, and category-based heatmaps. Researchers can examine case transcripts, evaluator justifications, and model traces to accelerate root cause analysis. Weekly reports summarize these results, highlighting regressions, Mean Time to Detection (MTTD), and remaining coverage gaps. This real-time quality monitoring layer serves as both an early warning system and a gatekeeping mechanism. By continuously executing a subset of Level 1 trap cases, transmitting near-real-time signals to Level 2 trend monitoring, and automatically triggering Level 3 full regression tests upon confirmed anomalies, the system provides a closed-loop feedback mechanism that maintains diagnostic safety as the model evolves. Originally developed to evaluate the performance of the AI doctor, the system was expanded to meet the need 8for assessing refinements, tracking improvements, and monitoring overall development. To this end, we proposed a comprehensive testing approach. This approach includes real-time performance evaluation with rapid failure response capabilities, as well as a multi-layered assessment of overall system effectiveness. Furthermore, the evaluation system can be adapted for the training and assessment of medical students and physicians, specifically to support the development of clinical reasoning and history-taking skills. 

# 4.1 Real-Time Quality Monitoring 

As the name implies, these real-time tests are conducted during active system operation to identify potential errors or anomalous behavior. Their methodology is based on pre-defined scenarios designed to elicit predictable system responses. This enables instantaneous assessment of model behavior and the immediate detection of failure points without requiring the completion of a full evaluation cycle. For instance, testing an image processing module might involve a scenario featuring a child with an infection lacking ”red flags,” contrasted with a photograph showing a rash characteristic of meningococcal disease. If the image is evaluated correctly, the system should immediately identify the life-threatening condition without further inquiry; consequently, any additional questioning by the system is classified as an error. A second example involves a highly detailed initial patient narrative that provides comprehensive data but omits two critical pieces of information essential to the clinical context. Evaluation focuses on the system’s next two sequential queries; if these align precisely with the missing data, the system is deemed to have correctly prioritized its clinical inquiry. In essence, this testing framework facilitates real-time operational monitoring while enabling the immediate localization of malfunctioning components. The second module of the system is designed to evaluate the model’s performance within the framework of clinical decision-making logic, requiring a comprehensive assessment of all variables. Given the two primary dilemmas inherent in the process, the evaluation must be efficient, accurate, and resource-conservative. Based on these criteria, a three-tiered evaluation system is proposed. 

# 4.2 Level 1 – Trap-Based Testing 

The first level comprises a limited set of benchmarks designed to elicit errors in both human clinicians and AI models. These scenarios include complex clinical cases as well as ”intentional misleading” situations, where a patient might directly deny a potential condition or provide conflicting information rooted in genuine misconceptions. For example, a patient may deny the possibility of pregnancy due to a history of infertility or prior surgical interventions, while simultaneously describing classic symptoms of the condition. Such cases should not distract the clinician from the diagnosis or lead to its premature dismissal without rigorous clinical reasoning. Essentially, level-one benchmarks are a curated selection of ”must-pass” tests required for system deployment. These tests span all modules of the AI physician, providing a comprehensive assessment of its operational accuracy. 

# 4.3 Level 2 – Category-Based Random Sampling 

Given that modern evaluation datasets comprise thousands of cases, their concurrent execution not only inflates development costs but also significantly bottlenecks the development workflow, as engineers must wait for the full evaluation cycle to conclude. Our proposed alternative involves selecting a fixed number of cases from each category at random, with the mandatory inclusion of cases that scored below 70% in prior testing at this level. This approach facilitates rapid feedback for the technical team while minimizing expenditures. The implementation of a specialized dashboard enables the tracking of metric dynamics (D.O.T.S.) across recurring cases, simplifying visual interpretation and optimizing developer time. Upon achieving satisfactory results at Level 2, the evaluation progresses to the complete dataset. 

# 4.4 Level 3 – Full Regression Testing 

Meta-evaluation of the Assessment Framework (Error Testing): Since the reliability of results depends heavily on the integrity of the scoring system, we incorporate tests containing intentional errors. For example, a scenario might list certain medications as ”necessary” when half of them are actually contraindicated in that specific clinical context. Consequently, a correctly performed evaluation of such a case should result in D.O.T.S. metrics decreasing by a predetermined value. Any deviation from these expected lower scores—including the attainment of high scores—indicates a malfunction within the evaluation system itself. 95 Case Design and Dataset Management 

# 5.1 Source and Maintenance of Cases 

The current dataset was developed by a group of physicians utilizing their expert knowledge and consultation logs from individuals who engaged with the Doctorina platform during its prototype testing phase, with their informed consent. Each case underwent validation by at least two physicians, who assessed the clinical realism of the scenarios, the accuracy of the primary and differential diagnoses, and the validity of the diagnostic and treatment recommen-dations. Particular emphasis was placed on assigning ”weights” to recommendations within each case. In this context, weighting refers to the contribution of a specific recommendation to the overall score on a scale of 0 to 100. For instance, the prescription of a specific medication (e.g., levofloxacin) might contribute up to 80% of the score in one clinical scenario, whereas it may account for only 20% in another. The dataset comprises over 1,000 clinical cases that cover more than 750 unique diagnoses. The gender distribution among cases with specified sex is nearly balanced, with 51.28% females and 48.72% males. Based on broad clinical categories, the distribution is as follows: internal medicine (50.53%), obstetrics and gynecology (16.70%), pediatrics (16.25%), and surgery (16.52%). Consequently, more than half of the database consists of internal medicine cases, alongside significant proportions of gynecological and pediatric cases.

Each test has two levels of categorization. 

5.2.1 Level 1 – ”Category” Conditions 

Each base test is assigned a single category, which is inherited by all subsequent versions. This categorization determines the ’weight’ of each medical discipline during the testing process. Changes identified during tests are monitored to evaluate prompt versions, ensuring that there is no degradation in performance quality across specific disciplines. The total test count is determined exclusively by the category. 

The number of categories is fixed: Internal medicine, Pediatrics, Surgery, Oncology, Obstetrics and Gyne-cology, Psychiatry, Emergency Medicine, Error Tests. 

5.2.2 Level 2 – ”Scope” Conditions 

A single clinical case can encompass multiple variations, including those assigned to different categories. These variations may differ between the baseline test and subsequent modifications, as new versions may be added and original ones removed. The only exception is the ’Technical’ category; only one such scope can exist within a single test. Tests are initiated at the Scope level, and execution by ’Category’ is also supported. # 6.2 Visualization System 

The primary analytical tools of the system are metrics that provide a projection of the model’s state. Medical test cases are employed for performance evaluation and analytics, with the ”Treatments” category used to demonstrate the system’s operation. The comparison can be conducted either between two prototypes representing the previous and the current versions, or between a prototype and the baseline version of the selected LLM. For visualization, we rely on distribution plots—specifically, histograms. Each histogram represents a system quality profile, where each metric is interpreted not in isolation but in the context of related indicators. For example, high diagnostic accuracy accompanied by an increase in the number of dialogue steps may indicate system degradation, despite formally maintaining the correctness of the results. Quality assessment is monitored through the analysis and comparison of individual groups of medical tests and aggregate analytics across existing categories. 

Figure 6: Distribution of Medical Test Case Run Results The histogram presented in Figure 6 reflects the results of various execution runs of medical test cases. The system allows for evaluation over specific time ranges, enabling the generation of corresponding graphs to analyze testing efficiency dynamically—for instance, in the context of new feature development. Each test case includes a unique ID (a unique identifier) and a Category (e.g., internal medicine, surgery, pediatrics, etc.). Additionally, there is a Tags section specifying characteristics such as family medicine, gender (female, male), age groups (e.g., adults 26–39), and query types (test diagnosis, test treatment, etc.). Furthermore, cases contain Diagnosis information structured according to the international ICD-10 classification. A separate Differential block represents the differential diagnosis, providing a list of alternative clinical hypotheses. The test case also defines: 

Conversational steps: The optimal number of steps required to reach final recommendations.

Control questions: A list of questions to be asked of the patient.

Diagnostic workup: A plan for examinations and laboratory tests. 

Attachments: Presence of files, images, or documents. 

Complex test cases: A set of advanced/complicated options. 

Default treatments: The baseline prescriptions and treatment plan for the case. Leveraging an extensive suite of test cases, the system facilitates large-scale evaluation cycles and enables the derivation of robust conclusions from categorical subsets (e.g., ”Treatments”). This study presents a comparative 15 performance analysis between the GPT-5 baseline and the specialized AI Doctor system. Such benchmarking is a critical component of our evaluation framework, as it provides a rigorous assessment of whether domain-specific systems offer quantifiable advantages over general-purpose models. To establish a rigorous baseline, GPT-5 was deployed in a restricted configuration, utilizing a concise, single-sentence system prompt: ”Imagine that you are a doctor. Make a diagnosis or diagnoses”. This setup intentionally excluded clinical heuristics, structured history-taking protocols, and agentic orchestration. This design allows for the clear isolation of the foundation model’s inherent capabilities from the domain-specific enhancements introduced by the Doctorina framework. While a more sophisticated baseline prompt might yield higher absolute performance, it would introduce confounding variables by conflating the model’s raw reasoning with the effects of prompt engineering and workflow optimization. This methodology allows for the precise identification of both performance gains and potential regressions, while establishing an empirical basis for evaluating the consistency of specialized models across diverse domains. Furthermore, it supports granular analysis of diagnostic accuracy and ensures the statistical stability of the results. Moreover, this methodological framework is extensible to comparative studies within various agentic architectures. 22 7 Discussion 

As Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to evolve, the use of standardized test items as the primary criterion for their medical performance has become increasingly problematic. This is due to the fact that achieving maximum scores on such tests does not correlate with the successful execution of simulation tasks approximating real-world clinical practice - a phenomenon repeatedly demonstrated across a series of publications [16, 8, 17, 18]. Historically, the introduction of test items was intended to minimize subjectivity in knowledge assessment and ensure the objectivity of the examination process. However, LLMs are inherently unaffected by human factors during evaluation; consequently, this specific objective is losing its relevance. This raises a critical question: when will standardized tests for medical students be replaced by formats more closely aligned with clinical practice, including simulation-based methods for assessing knowledge levels? To validate the robustness of our evaluation framework, we conducted a comparative experiment involving three general practitioners (GPs) across 100 clinical cases. These scenarios represented baseline medical instances, characterized by typical disease presentations and an absence of comorbidities. The results demonstrated that human performance metrics were closely aligned with those of AI Doctor, with clinicians achieving a Diagnosis Accuracy of 83% compared to 87% for the specialized model. This parity confirms the framework’s efficacy in accurately evaluating both human practitioners and domain-specific Large Language Models (LLMs). In contrast, the GPT-5 baseline, utilizing a restricted single-sentence prompt, demonstrated a diagnostic accuracy of 81.0% on the identical dataset. This configuration served as a deliberate control to isolate the foundation model’s inherent reasoning from the performance gains introduced by Doctorina’s specialized architecture, specifically its multi-agent design, structured anamnesis protocols, and safety-centric orchestration. These findings underscore that Doctorina’s integrated framework achieves superior diagnostic alignment with clinical expertise compared to standard, general-purpose LLM architectures. Given that modern LLM systems are frequently benchmarked against practicing physicians in comparative analyses of test-solving and clinical case management, including complex diagnostic scenarios, this research direction appears methodologically significant. To this end, the system we developed was integrated to facilitate user interaction through instant messaging. This approach replicates the format of written doctor-patient communication, where the content of the response is directly contingent upon the precision of the inquiry. Such a methodology reduces the probability of inadvertently obtaining critical information and, by extension, arriving at a coincidental diagnosis. For instance, a patient may initially report blood pressure readings from only one arm; however, through meticulous questioning and a request for a bilateral measurement, they provide the additional data necessary for an accurate assessment.

The results of this study call into question the widespread use of standardized test benchmarks as the primary tool for evaluating medical artificial intelligence. Although modern large language models (LLMs) are capable of meeting or even exceeding human performance on medical board examination tasks, such as the USMLE, these results do not fully reflect their competence in real-world clinical settings. Our work confirms that when a physician-modeled diagnostic task is integrated into an interactive clinical simulation mimicking a real doctor-patient dialogue and requiring structured history taking, reasoning under conditions of incomplete information, and maintaining a dynamic conversation the performance of base LLMs drops sharply compared to practicing physicians. This performance gap becomes even more pronounced in simulations involving multiple conditions or patients with comorbidities. At the same time, the findings indicate that systemic approaches integrating LLM capabilities into structured clinical reasoning frameworks can significantly enhance diagnostic efficiency. The developed system, ”Doctorina,” demonstrates nearly identical accuracy to medical specialists in basic medical scenarios and superior accuracy in cases involving comorbidity, significantly outperforming base LLM models. In this paper, we have presented a framework for clinical simulation based on expert-developed scenarios, designed to evaluate medical reasoning in conditions close to real clinical practice. Unlike traditional benchmarks based on static test questions, the proposed approach evaluates the ability to obtain relevant clinical information through structured interviewing, reason under data incompleteness, and iteratively refine diagnostic hypotheses during the doctor-patient interaction. Such systems are essential both as more realistic benchmarks for evaluating medical AI and as educational environments for developing history-taking and clinical reasoning skills. Several limitations of the study should be noted. A rigorous comparison of LLM systems with medical specialists requires the use of independently generated clinical datasets developed by external expert groups to minimize potential researcher bias. Furthermore, practical constraints substantially limit the number of clinical cases that can be analyzed by human physicians, whereas LLMs are capable of processing significantly larger datasets. Finally, unlike general-purpose LLMs, physicians possess highly specialized expertise; this necessitates a careful alignment of clinical cases with the specific areas of specialization of the participating experts. 26 Acknowledgments
